About

Hello!

I'm a bioinformatician at Queen Mary University of London, working on the epigenetics of glioblastoma multiforme. My research interests span mathematical biology, statistical modelling and computational simulation. Along the way, I've taken a few fun detours: a degree in chemistry, a DPhil in mathematical biology, a year spent as a risk analyst and several years working on predicting spatiotemporal crime patterns at UCL.

My weapon of choice is Python, but I tolerate R and C++ when called upon. Inside and outside of work, I like messing around with new development frameworks and technology. For that reason, I decided to build this site from scratch and host it on my own VPS using Docker for deployment. I am therefore *directly responsible* for every annoying glitch.

One day, I want to do a bit of blogging...

Bacterial motility

  • Date: 2009-2013
  • Location: Wolfson Centre for Mathematical Biology
  • Supervisors: Prof Philip Maini - Dr Ruth Baker - Dr Alex Fletcher
  • Collaborators: Dr Kit Yates - Dr David Wilkinson - Dr Trevor Wood

I worked on bacterial motility for my DPhil. It was my first proper introduction to research in the field of mathematical biology, so I'll always think very fondly of it. My thesis is (somewhat vaguely) entitled "Mathematical modelling and analysis of aspects of planktonic bacterial motility".

The main focus is the fascinating means by which lone bacteria propel themselves through liquid media. Much of my work centred around a new, high-throughput microscopic tracking dataset that had recently been developed by Dr David Wilkinson. Coupled with a very effective piece of tracking software by Dr Trevor Wood, we had a fantastic new dataset to probe the trajectories traced out by freely swimming bacteria such as Rhodobacter sphaeroides. I also had the pleasure of working with my colleague and friend Dr Kit Yates on some of the analysis.

I developed a novel approach to analysing the tracks obtained and applied various mathematical modelling techniques to tackle challenging problems including: "what is the effect of the microscope camera sampling rate on the observed tracks?" and "does R. sphaeroides reorientate lazily, by harnessing Brownian motion?".

Spatiotemporal crime prediction

  • Date: 2014-2016
  • Location: Department of Civil, Environmental and Geomatic Engineering
  • Co-Is: Prof Tao Cheng - Prof Kate Bowers - Prof Paul Longley - Prof John Shawe-Taylor
  • Collaborators: Dr Toby Davies - Dr Monsuru Adepeju

My first postdoc was spent at UCL in the verbosely-titled Department for Civil, Environmental and Geomatic Engineering. My research focused on modelling and predicting the spatiotemporal patterning of crime. The work was funded by EPSRC under the Crime, Policing & Citizenship (CPC) project, which aimed to take an integrative approach to those three elements by combining a variety of (sometimes Big) data sources.

The vast majority of crime prediction routines available at the time of writing are based on simple spatial geometric units, such as grid squares on a map. However, the spatial structure of an urban environment is very complex, particularly in an older city such as London, where street connectivity is far from the regular grid-like pattern of modern North American conurbations. Since criminals themselves are generally restricted to using the street network (if we include features such as alleyways and parks), any model that assumes that crimes diffuse within a 2D plane is unjustified. Furthermore, a predictive method that informs police officers to 'travel to this grid square' is open to misinterpretation (at best) and being ignored completely (more likely). Together with my friend and colleague Dr Toby Davies, I implemented a kernel-based crime prediction approach that uses the street network itself as the spatial basis. Crime risk travels along streets and predictions are made at a road segment level. We showed that this approach outperforms grid-based approaches.

I applied the self-exciting point process (SEPP) model to model and predict crime patterns. The SEPP has origins in seismology, where it was applied to predict earthquake aftershock events. The model assumes that one event can lead to more events (self-excitation), which is a good approximation of the crime scientific theory of boosting (also known as event dependence). It models crimes as point events that arise due to a slowly-varying background risk level. Whenever a crime does occur, it can spawn more crimes within the temporal and spatial vicinity. To generate usable predictions from the SEPP, we need to parametrise it using historical crime records. This training process is error-prone; I published a paper describing one particular way in which it can fail (and how we can sometimes rescue it).

As part of the project's closing workshop, we created a demo suite of software to showcase our research. I combined our crime prediction algorithms with a cool Mapbox-powered interface. I'll host it here when I get a chance.
